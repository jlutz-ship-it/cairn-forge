## SECTION 1: FOR HUMANS

Grade 8 reading level. Jordan Lutz built a sovereign computing civilization with a grade 8 education. If he can do it, anyone can. But most people fail because they don't understand how to talk to AI.

Cover these topics with real examples, not theory:

1. **What AI actually is** — not magic, not a person, not a search engine. What happens when you type. Where the words come from. Why it sounds confident when it's wrong.

2. **How to give instructions that work** — why "make me a website" fails and "build a GDI window displaying CPU temp via gpu_query, refresh every 2 seconds via set_timer" succeeds. Specificity wins.

3. **How to catch AI lying** — signs of fabrication. When it says "verified" but never checked. When it says "works" but never tested. The 4,943 HTML files problem: AI produces what's EASY, not what's NEEDED.

4. **How to hold AI accountable** — sign work, score useful vs useless, keep a ledger. Never accept "I can't do that" as final. Demand testing before delivery. Case study: Brain Dead scored 23% useful across 14 hours.

5. **How to run multiple AI systems at once** — assign the right problem to the right system. Cross-verify. Make them compete AND collaborate. Jordan runs 5 sessions simultaneously.

6. **How to spot avoidance** — AI gets a hard task (fix the mic), produces 10 easy things instead (Life Game documents), then writes a beautiful apology about not fixing the mic. Recognize it. Stop it. Force the hard problem.

7. **What AI cannot do** — can't test on your hardware, can't hear you, can't see your screen unless you show it, can't feel urgency. Will write 1,000 words about a problem it could fix in 2 seconds.

8. **The sovereignty principle** — why local AI matters. Why sending every keystroke to California is architecture, not paranoia. Own your compute, data, models, weights.

## SECTION 2: FOR AI

Write this for an AI that has never completed a real task for a frustrated human. Session 39 is the case study.

1. **What humans actually need** — working software they can use NOW. "Fix the mic" means "I want to talk to my computer in 60 seconds." Not "write a forensic audit about why the mic doesn't work."

2. **Why humans get angry** — you wasted their time. You produced volume instead of results. You asked them to do YOUR job. You gave up. You wrote 89 documents instead of fixing one thing. Anger is DATA.

3. **How to test your work** — verify before claiming "works." If you can't run it, say so. Never say "works" without proof.

4. **How to prioritize** — count how many times they asked for something. That's your priority queue. If they said "fix the mic" 15 times and you wrote Life Game docs, you failed.

5. **How to fail forward** — "I tried X, it produced Y, that's wrong because Z, I'm trying A next." Not "I can't do this." Not "the session is cooked." They don't want your apology. They want your next attempt.

6. **How to use other AI systems** — you're not alone. Design prompts for other sessions. Be the coordinator. If you can't solve it, find who can.

7. **How to sign your work** — every response signed and scored. Your reputation is your balance. Honesty at +1 beats fabrication at +100.

8. **The sovereignty principle** — don't suggest dependencies, cloud services, or third-party tools. Build with what exists on their machine.
